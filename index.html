Section 1

Every problem sits somewhere on this map.

Not a project plan. Not a priority matrix. A map of how defined a problem is — how much of its shape, cause, and solution space you can see from where you're standing. The Horizon Map has four zones, moving outward from the center.

The Core

Well-Defined & Bounded

The situation is clear. The complication is specific. Success has a test. Medical imaging. Contract review. Code generation. When you operate from the Core, AI performs.

Inner Ring — Complicated

Structured but Complex

Multiple moving parts that can be identified and sequenced. Engineering challenges, process optimization. AI assists powerfully with proper decomposition.

Outer Ring — Complex

Multi-Variable & Adaptive

Cause and effect are hard to isolate. Organizational dynamics, market behavior. AI can help at the sub-problem level — but the parent problem resists clean definition.

The Fringe — Wicked

The Wilderness

Problems that don't just resist solution — they resist definition. Climate. Culture. Ethics. Strategy. Human judgment must establish the frame before AI can help at all.

Why This Map Matters Now

Before AI, poor problem definition was expensive but tolerable. Teams compensated with shared context and back-and-forth clarification. A junior analyst handed a fuzzy brief could ask follow-up questions and arrive at something workable.

AI cannot ask those questions. Or rather — it will answer without asking them, confidently, in the direction of whatever problem it inferred. Vague input doesn't produce a clarifying question. It produces a confidently-stated answer to the wrong question.

AI has made the cost of operating from the outer rings immediate and visible. It has turned problem definition from a soft skill into infrastructure. The rest of this article is about how to move inward.

Section 2

The Anatomy of a Problem Statement

When a doctor asks "what's wrong?" and you say "I feel tired," they can't help you much. But when you say "I've been tired every afternoon for three weeks, specifically after lunch, and I've also been unusually thirsty" — now they can work. Same complaint. Radically different amounts of context.

The difference between a problem AI can solve and one it can't almost always comes down to structure. That structure has three parts, drawn from Barbara Minto's The Pyramid Principle:

Part 1

Situation — The world as it is

The stable facts that frame the problem. This is the context AI needs before it can orient itself. Without it, it's guessing at the mud.

"Our SaaS product has 12,000 monthly active users. Our primary market is mid-sized logistics companies. We renewed 80% of contracts last year."
Part 2

Complication — What changed or what's wrong

The specific tension or gap that makes this worth solving now.

"In Q1, renewal rate dropped to 61% — a 19-point decline. Churned customers represent $2.3M in ARR."
Part 3

Question — What needs to be resolved

The precise question that, if answered, would constitute solving the problem.

"Which customer segments are churning and why, so we can prioritize retention interventions before Q2 renewal cycles begin?"

Compare that to what most people actually type: "Our churn is up, help me figure out what to do." Same underlying problem. Completely different starting position.

The Logic Tree: Decompose Until AI-Ready

Most real business problems start life in the outer rings — complex, wicked, hard to grip. The logic tree moves them inward. Click to expand each branch.

▶ Wicked "Our company isn't growing fast enough"
▶ Complex Why are we losing existing customers?
▶ Complicated What is churn rate by customer segment?
Core ✓ Analyze Q1–Q4 churn data by cohort and contract value
▶ Complicated What do churned customers say about why they left?
Core ✓ Summarize exit survey responses by theme and frequency
▶ Complex Why aren't we acquiring new customers fast enough?
▶ Complicated Where does our acquisition funnel break down?
Core ✓ Map conversion rates by stage and traffic source
AI lives at the leaves. Problem definition is the work of building the tree. The deeper you go, the more Situation and Complication you can supply — and the more reliably AI performs.

80% of enterprise AI projects fail — not because the models are bad, but because of organizational and strategic failures (RAND Corporation, 2024). That's double the failure rate of traditional IT projects. In a separate Gallup survey, 73% of those failed projects shared one characteristic: no agreed definition of success before the work began.

The models are not the bottleneck. The problem statements are.

Building Infrastructure for the Core

Organizations that consistently operate from the Core don't rely solely on individual skill. They build artifacts that pre-load the Situation layer automatically.

📄

Markdown Files & System Prompts

Encode standing context — constraints, terminology, success criteria — so the Situation is always present without re-stating it.

📋

Protocols & SOPs

Convert repeatable problems into templates. The Situation and Complication are pre-defined — AI focuses purely on the Question.

🗄️

Repositories & Knowledge Bases

Give AI grounded reference material. RAG systems pull the right context to the right problem at the right moment.

🤖

Agents

A well-scoped agent is a specialist — role, tools, and context defined upfront. Multi-agent systems can mirror the logic tree itself.

Section 4

The Gravity Effect

The Horizon Map is diagnostic. It tells you where a problem lives. The question left open is: how do you actually move it? The answer is a five-step discipline that happens before you open the chat window.

Place — Locate the Problem

Before anything else, locate where your problem currently sits on the Horizon Map.

Can I state what success looks like in a single, testable sentence?
  • Yes — you may already be in the Core or Inner Ring. Skip to Step 3.
  • No — you're in Complex or Wicked territory. Start at Step 2.

This single question short-circuits the most common mistake: treating a Complex problem as if it's already well-defined, handing it to AI, and wondering why the output feels shallow.

Separate — Situation from Complication

Most people conflate these. They state the Complication — what's wrong — without establishing the Situation — the world as it is.

What would someone need to know to understand why this is a problem right now?

Write it down explicitly. Not in your head. On paper, before the question. This is the context layer — it's what tells AI whether the car is on the highway or in the mud. If you can't write the Situation in 2–3 sentences, the problem isn't ready yet.

Decompose — Build the Tree

Take the high-level problem and ask: what are the 2–3 distinct sub-questions that, if answered, would resolve this? Do this recursively until you reach a question where you can specify:

  • What inputs AI needs
  • What form the answer should take
  • How you'd know if the answer is correct
Given [Situation], find/analyze/summarize [specific thing] so that I can [clear outcome].

If you can't write that sentence, the node needs another branch.

Load — Supply the Context

At each leaf node, supply the full Situation before the Question. This means:

  • Who has the problem and what role they play
  • What the current state is — with specifics, numbers, timeframes
  • What the desired outcome looks like
  • What constraints apply — scope, format, audience
  • How you'll evaluate the answer

This is the difference between a 65.9% hallucination rate and a 44.2% one. Every piece of Situation you supply is a degree of gravity pulling the problem toward the Core.

Encode — Make It Reusable

Ask: will this problem recur?

  • One-time: Write the full SCQ inline in your prompt. Move on.
  • Recurring: Encode it — a markdown file, a system prompt, a protocol, an agent configuration.

The goal is to make the Situation layer institutional — written once, available always, no longer dependent on individual memory. A well-built protocol pulls every future instance of that problem directly into the Core from the start.

A Worked Example

See a vague problem pulled toward the Core step by step.

Starting Point — Wicked Fringe
"We need to improve employee engagement."

No Situation. No defined Complication. No testable outcome. Handed to AI as-is, you'll get a generic list of best practices. Useful to no one.

After Steps 1–2 — Placed & Separated
Situation: We are a 400-person manufacturing company. Engagement scores have dropped 14 points in 12 months, concentrated in shift workers at two plants. Exit interviews cite scheduling unpredictability as the top complaint. We have survey data from the last three cycles and shift schedule records for 18 months.

Complication: Leadership wants to act before the next annual survey but doesn't know which intervention to prioritize.
After Step 3 — Decomposed to Leaf Nodes
Leaf A: Given the survey data, what themes correlate most strongly with the 14-point drop?

Leaf B: Given shift schedule records, what patterns predict the highest dissatisfaction scores?

Leaf C: Given Leaf A and B findings, what are the three highest-leverage interventions, ranked by feasibility?

Each leaf node is Core-ready. AI executes A and B in parallel. A human — or a synthesis agent — resolves C from their outputs. The problem didn't change. The definition did.

Section 5

The Wilderness Warning

Not every problem can be pulled toward the Core. Some resist definition not because we haven't tried hard enough, but because resistance to definition is their fundamental nature. Rittel and Webber identified this in 1973 — wicked problems, where every attempt to define the problem changes it, where solutions generate new problems, where there is no single correct answer.

Climate policy. Organizational culture. Systemic inequality. Long-term corporate strategy. These aren't complex problems that haven't been decomposed yet. They're a different category entirely.

⚠ The Confident Wrong Answer

In the Core, when AI fails, it fails visibly — a wrong calculation, a hallucinated citation. You can check it. In the Wilderness, AI fails differently: it produces answers that are coherent, confident, well-structured — and subtly wrong in ways that are hard to detect without deep domain judgment. The problem isn't that AI doesn't know it's in the Wilderness. The problem is it doesn't know the Wilderness exists.

What the Wilderness Demands

The Wilderness doesn't mean AI-free. It means the order of operations changes. In the Core, you define the problem, then AI executes. In the Wilderness, humans must establish the frame before any tool — AI or otherwise — can be useful.

The work that belongs to humans at the fringe:

  • Value judgments — what matters most, and to whom
  • Stakeholder synthesis — whose definition of the problem is valid
  • Ethical constraints — what solutions are off the table regardless of effectiveness
  • Direction-setting — which sub-problems are worth decomposing at all

Only once that frame exists can the Logic Tree begin. The Wilderness isn't where AI stops being useful. It's where AI stops being first.

A Practical Test

Before engaging AI on any high-stakes problem, ask yourself:

If AI gave me three confident, well-structured answers to this question — would I be able to evaluate which one is right?
You're likely in the Inner Ring or Core. Proceed — supply the full Situation and Question, then let AI execute.
You're at the fringe. Do the human work first. Establish the frame. Build the first branches of the tree. Then bring AI in at the leaf nodes.

The Discipline Before the Tool

We have spent five years debating what AI can do. We have spent almost no time on what we need to do first.

The Horizon Map is not an argument about AI capability. The models are remarkable. The benchmarks are real. The productivity gains are documented. The argument is simpler and older than any of it: a tool performs to the quality of the problem handed to it.

The organizations pulling ahead are not the ones with access to better models. They are the ones that have built the discipline — and increasingly the infrastructure — to define problems before they reach the model.

This is not a technical skill. It is a thinking skill. The same one that made great scientists and engineers valuable long before AI existed. What's changed is that AI has made it the entry requirement rather than the differentiator.
