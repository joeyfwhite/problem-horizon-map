Section 1

Every problem sits somewhere on this map.

Not a project plan. Not a priority matrix. A map of how defined a problem is, showing how much of its shape, cause, and solution space you can see from where you're standing. Four zones, moving outward from the center.

The Core

Well-Defined & Bounded

The situation is clear. The complication is specific. Success has a test. Medical imaging. Contract review. Code generation. When you operate from the Core, AI performs.

Inner Ring — Complicated

Structured but Multi-Part

Multiple moving parts that can be identified and sequenced. Engineering challenges, process optimization. AI assists powerfully here with proper decomposition.

Outer Ring — Complex

Multi-Variable & Adaptive

Cause and effect are hard to isolate. Organizational dynamics, market behavior. AI helps at the sub-problem level, but the parent problem resists clean definition.

The Fringe — Wicked

The Wilderness

Problems that resist definition entirely. Climate. Culture. Ethics. Strategy. Human judgment must establish the frame before AI can execute. For exploratory problems, AI can still contribute as a thinking partner.

Why This Map Matters Now

Before AI, poor problem definition was expensive but tolerable. Teams compensated with shared context and follow-up questions. A junior analyst with a fuzzy brief could ask three clarifying questions and arrive at something workable.

AI cannot ask those questions. Rather, it will answer without asking them, confidently, in the direction of whatever problem it inferred. Vague input doesn't produce a clarifying question. It produces a confidently-stated answer to the wrong question.

AI has made the cost of operating from the outer rings immediate and visible. It has turned problem definition from a soft skill into infrastructure.

One caveat worth naming upfront: for software development specifically, agentic tools like Codex and Claude Code already compress much of this work. They decompose tasks, ask clarifying questions, and iterate toward a deliverable because code has a built-in truth test. It either runs or it does not.

Knowledge work is different. Strategy, analysis, research, and diagnosis do not resolve into a passing test suite. The process of exploration is itself valuable, and problem definitions shift as understanding grows. This article is written for that territory: managers, analysts, and knowledge workers navigating problems where the map, not the model, is the limiting factor. The rest of this article is about how to move inward.

Section 2

The Anatomy of a Problem Statement

When a doctor asks "what's wrong?" and you say "I feel tired," they can't help you much. But when you say "I've been tired every afternoon for three weeks, specifically after lunch, and I've also been unusually thirsty," they can actually work with that. Same complaint. Radically different amounts of context.

A well-formed problem statement has three parts, drawn from Barbara Minto's The Pyramid Principle:

Part 1

Situation — The world as it is

The stable facts that frame the problem. This is the context AI needs before it can orient itself. Without it, it's guessing at the mud.

"Our SaaS product has 12,000 monthly active users. Our primary market is mid-sized logistics companies. We renewed 80% of contracts last year."
Part 2

Complication — What changed or what's wrong

The specific tension or gap that makes this worth solving now.

"In Q1, renewal rate dropped to 61%, a 19-point decline. Churned customers represent $2.3M in ARR."
Part 3

Question — What needs to be resolved

The precise question that, if answered, would constitute solving the problem.

"Which customer segments are churning and why, so we can prioritize retention interventions before Q2 renewal cycles begin?"

Compare that to what most people actually type: "Our churn is up, help me figure out what to do." Same underlying problem. Completely different starting position.

Situation: [stable context — what is true about our world right now]
Complication: [what changed, or what gap now exists]
Question: [what needs resolution, and what a good answer looks like]

What the Data Says

80% of enterprise AI projects fail, not because the models are bad, but because of organizational and strategic failures (RAND Corporation, 2024). That's double the failure rate of traditional IT projects. In a separate Gallup survey, 73% of those failed projects shared one characteristic: no agreed definition of success before the work began.

The models are not the bottleneck. The problem statements are.

Building Infrastructure for the Core

Organizations that consistently operate from the Core build artifacts that pre-load the Situation layer automatically, so every problem arrives pre-situated rather than depending on individual discipline each time.

📄

Markdown Files & System Prompts

Encode standing context (constraints, terminology, success criteria) so the Situation is always present without re-stating it.

📋

Protocols & SOPs

Convert repeatable problems into templates. The Situation and Complication are pre-defined, and AI focuses purely on the Question.

🤖

Section 4

The Gravity Effect

The Horizon Map is diagnostic. It tells you where a problem lives. The question left open is: how do you actually move it? Five steps, all taken before you open the chat window. For exploratory problems, these steps are iterative rather than sequential. You may pass through them more than once as the problem clarifies.

Place — Locate the Problem on the Map

Before anything else, locate where your problem currently sits on the Horizon Map.

Can I state what success looks like in a single, testable sentence?
  • Yes: you may already be in the Core or Inner Ring. Skip to Step 3.
  • No: you're in Complex or Wicked territory. Start at Step 2.

This short-circuits the most common mistake: treating a Complex problem as if it's already well-defined, handing it to AI, and wondering why the output feels shallow.

Separate — Situation from Complication

Most people conflate these. They state the Complication (what's wrong) without establishing the Situation (the world as it is).

What would someone need to know to understand why this is a problem right now?

Write it down explicitly. Not in your head. This is the context layer: it tells AI whether the car is on the highway or in the mud. If you can't write the Situation in 2–3 sentences, the problem isn't ready yet.

Decompose — Build the Tree

Take the high-level problem and ask: what are the 2–3 distinct sub-questions that, if answered, would resolve this? Do this recursively until you can specify:

  • What inputs AI needs
  • What form the answer should take
  • How you'd know if the answer is correct
Given [Situation], find/analyze/summarize [specific thing] so that I can [clear outcome].

If you can't write that sentence, the node needs another branch.

Load — Supply the Full Context

At each leaf node, supply the full Situation before the Question:

  • Who has the problem and what role they play
  • What the current state is: specifics, numbers, timeframes
  • What the desired outcome looks like
  • What constraints apply: scope, format, audience
  • How you'll evaluate the answer

This is the difference between a 65.9% hallucination rate and a 44.2% one. Every piece of Situation is a degree of gravity pulling the problem toward the Core.

Encode — Make It Reusable

Ask: will this problem recur?

  • One-time: Write the full SCQ inline in your prompt. Move on.
  • Recurring: Encode it as a markdown file, system prompt, protocol, or agent configuration.

A well-built protocol pulls every future instance of that problem directly into the Core from the start. The Situation layer becomes institutional, not personal.

A Worked Example

See a vague problem pulled toward the Core step by step.

Starting Point — Wicked Fringe
"We need to improve employee engagement."

No Situation. No defined Complication. No testable outcome. Handed to AI as-is, you'll get a generic list of best practices useful to no one.

After Steps 1–2 — Placed & Separated
Situation: We are a 400-person manufacturing company. Engagement scores have dropped 14 points in 12 months, concentrated in shift workers at two plants. Exit interviews cite scheduling unpredictability as the top complaint. We have survey data from the last three cycles and shift schedule records for 18 months.

Complication: Leadership wants to act before the next annual survey but doesn't know which intervention to prioritize.
After Step 3 — Decomposed to Leaf Nodes
Leaf A: Given the survey data, what themes correlate most strongly with the 14-point drop?

Leaf B: Given shift schedule records, what patterns predict the highest dissatisfaction scores?

Leaf C: Given Leaf A & B findings, what are the three highest-leverage interventions ranked by feasibility?

Each leaf is Core-ready. AI executes A and B in parallel. A human, or a synthesis agent, resolves C. The problem didn't change. The definition did.

Section 5

The Wilderness Warning

Not every problem can be pulled toward the Core. Some resist definition not because we haven't tried hard enough, but because resistance to definition is their fundamental nature. Rittel and Webber identified this in 1973 as wicked problems, where every attempt to define the problem changes it and where solutions generate new problems.

Climate policy. Organizational culture. Systemic inequality. Long-term corporate strategy. These aren't complex problems that haven't been decomposed yet. They're a different category entirely.

⚠ The Confident Wrong Answer

In the Core, when AI fails, it fails visibly. In the Wilderness, AI produces answers that are coherent, confident, and well-structured but subtly wrong in ways that are hard to detect without deep domain judgment. The problem isn't that AI doesn't know it's in the Wilderness. The problem is it doesn't know the Wilderness exists.

What the Wilderness Demands

The Wilderness doesn't mean AI-free. It means the order of operations changes. In the Core, you define the problem, then AI executes. In the Wilderness, humans must establish the frame before any tool can be useful.

  • Value judgments: what matters most, and to whom
  • Stakeholder synthesis: whose definition of the problem is valid
  • Ethical constraints: what solutions are off the table regardless of effectiveness
  • Direction-setting: which sub-problems are even worth decomposing

The Wilderness isn't where AI stops being useful. It's where AI stops being first.

The Learning Loop Problem

There is a related category worth naming: problems that are not permanently wicked, but temporarily undefined because the definition only emerges through the work itself. Researching a new market. Developing a strategy in an unfamiliar domain. Diagnosing a systemic issue before the data is clear. These problems live in learning loops where you do not know the full shape of the problem until you have started working it, and every answer shifts the question.

For these, AI's most valuable role shifts from executor to thinking partner. Not "analyze this and return findings" but "what am I missing in this framing?" or "what would a rigorous skeptic say about this direction?" The output is not a deliverable. It is a clearer mind.

This is the version of the Wilderness where AI earns its place. Not by solving, but by helping you think well enough to define what solving would even mean.

A Practical Test

Before engaging AI on any high-stakes problem, ask yourself. Then pick the option that best describes where you are.

If AI gave me three confident, well-structured answers to this question, would I be able to evaluate which one is right?
You're in the Inner Ring or Core. Apply the Gravity Effect: supply the full Situation and Question, then let AI execute.
You're in the Wilderness. Do the human work first. Clarify whose values apply, what constraints are non-negotiable, and what direction you're heading. Then build the first branches of the tree and bring AI in at the leaf nodes.
You're in a learning loop. Don't ask AI for answers yet. Ask what you're missing, what assumptions are embedded in your framing, or what a rigorous skeptic would say. The goal right now is a clearer question, not a deliverable.

The Discipline Before the Tool

We have spent five years debating what AI can do. We have spent almost no time on what we need to do first.

The Horizon Map is not an argument about AI capability. The models are remarkable. The benchmarks are real. The productivity gains are documented. The argument is simpler: a tool performs to the quality of the problem handed to it. Always has.

That holds for the most capable agentic systems just as much as a basic chat window. The tools are getting faster, broader, and more autonomous. The discipline of knowing what to ask them remains yours.

The organizations pulling ahead are not the ones with access to better models. They are the ones that have built the discipline and the infrastructure to define problems before they reach the model.

This is not a technical skill. It is a thinking skill. What's changed is that AI has made it the entry requirement rather than the differentiator.
